Module 02 Asking Great Questions

Ask Before You Assume

The question you don't ask costs more than the one you do. Curiosity is not weakness — it's the fastest path to getting things right.

The Core Idea

Why assuming feels safer — and why it isn't

Assuming feels efficient. You get the instruction, you fill in the blanks, you start moving. No questions asked, no time lost. Except when the blanks get filled in wrong — and they often do — you've spent time solving the wrong problem.

The real cost of assuming isn't the mistake. It's the rework, the misalignment, the quiet erosion of trust when someone realizes you were guessing instead of asking. One well-placed question at the start saves hours of correction at the end.

"Asking a question doesn't signal that you don't know. It signals that you care enough to get it right."

People who ask great questions are not the ones who know the least. They're usually the ones who understand the most — because they've made a habit of closing the gap between what they think they know and what's actually true.

Understanding the Problem

Five reasons we assume instead of ask

We don't want to look uninformed

Asking a question can feel like admitting a gap. But guessing wrong and needing to redo the work looks far worse.

We think we already know

Past experience creates shortcuts. The problem is that this situation might not be the same as the last one — and the difference matters.

We don't want to bother anyone

We assume the other person is too busy. But a 30-second question now is always better than a 30-minute mistake later.

We don't know what to ask

Sometimes the gap isn't confidence — it's not knowing which question to ask. This module fixes that.

We're moving too fast

Speed feels productive. But rushing past a clarifying question is how smart people end up doing brilliant work on the wrong thing.

The Skill

What makes a question great

Not all questions are equal. A great question is open enough to invite a real answer, specific enough to get useful information, and purposeful — asked because you need to know, not because you're stalling.

The Tool

The three questions to ask before any task

The Before-You-Start Checklist

Ask these before you dive in — every time.

1 What does done look like? Don't assume you know what the finished product should be. Ask explicitly. "What does a good outcome look like for you?"
2 What's the deadline — and why? Knowing the real deadline (and the reason behind it) tells you how to pace your effort and what trade-offs are acceptable.
3 Is there anything I should know that I'm not asking about? This is the question most people never ask. It surfaces hidden context before it becomes a surprise.

Tomorrow's Takeaway

Before you start your next task — ask all three.

Don't begin until you can answer: What does done look like? What's the real deadline and why? What should I know that I haven't asked about? Takes 60 seconds. Saves hours.
