Own Your Gaps

Asking for what you need is a professional skill, not a weakness. Pretending you don't need anything is the real risk — to you and to the team.

The Core Idea

Why hiding your needs costs everyone

When you don't say what you need, two things happen. First, you don't get it. Second, the people around you can't do their jobs properly because they're working with incomplete information. A team where people hide their gaps isn't a strong team — it's a team where problems compound silently until they surface as emergencies.

Asking for what you need isn't a burden on others. It's how good work actually gets done. The professional who says "I need clarity on this before I go further" gets better results than the one who powers through uncertain and produces work that misses the mark.

"The people who advocate clearly for what they need aren't high-maintenance. They're the ones you can actually count on."

Understanding the Problem

Why we stay silent when we need something

Fear of looking incapable

Asking for help can feel like admitting you can't handle it. But the most capable people ask the most questions — because they know the stakes.

Not wanting to be a burden

You don't want to slow someone else down. But silence that causes a mistake slows everyone down far more.

Not knowing how to ask

Some people genuinely don't have the language. They feel the gap but don't know how to name it professionally.

Hoping it will resolve itself

The optimism that says "maybe it'll be fine." It usually isn't — and now the problem is bigger and closer to the deadline.

Past experience of being dismissed

If you've been shut down before, you stop asking. That's a learned response — and this module helps unlearn it.

The Language

How to ask for what you need — professionally

The difference between asking confidently and asking apologetically is the language you use. Here's the shift.

Apologetic (avoid) Professional (use this)
"Sorry, I know this is probably a dumb question, but..."
"I have a question before I move forward:"
"I hate to ask, but I'm kind of stuck..."
"I need some direction on this — can we take five minutes?"
"I don't know if this makes sense to ask for, but I need more time..."
"I need two more days on this to do it right."
"You're probably too busy, but if you get a chance..."
"I need fifteen minutes with you this week — when works?"
"I might be missing something obvious, but this doesn't make sense to me..."
"I need more context on this before I can move forward accurately."

The Tool

The Need Statement formula

Say It Simply and Directly

I need ___ so that I can ___.

1 Name the need specifically. Not "some help" — but what, exactly. More time? A decision? A resource? Clarity on one point?
2 Connect it to the outcome. "So that I can ___" shows this isn't personal — it's about the work. It turns your need into a shared interest.
3 Stop there. No over-explaining. No apologizing. You've said what you need and why it matters. Let the other person respond.

Example: "I need clarity on the budget limit so that I can give you three real options instead of guesses."

Example: "I need an extra day on this so that I can verify the numbers before they go to the client."
