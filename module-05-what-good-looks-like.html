Module 05 A Taste for Excellence

What Good Looks Like

Excellence isn't perfectionism. It's knowing the standard — and holding yourself to it before anyone checks your work.

~25 minTo complete
Read · Reflect · WriteFormat
1 TakeawayTo use tomorrow

The Core Idea

The gap between done and done well

Most work gets completed. Very little work is excellent. The gap isn't usually talent or effort — it's standard. The person who produces excellent work has a clear internal picture of what "good" looks like for this task, and they don't stop until they've reached it. That picture doesn't come from someone checking their work. It comes from them.

Excellence is a habit of self-measurement. It's the moment before you hand something over when you ask yourself: is this my best work, or just finished work? Those are not the same question, and the people who know the difference are the ones others want on their team.

"Done is not a standard. Done well is. Know the difference before you hand it over."

Two Standards

Completed vs. excellent — what each looks like

Completed

It's finished and handed over

Technically done. All boxes checked. No obvious errors. Submitted on time. The person receiving it can work with it — but you know it could have been sharper.

Excellent

It's finished and it's right

Done — and you'd be proud to put your name on it. You caught the thing that almost slipped. You gave it ten more minutes. It does exactly what it needs to do, clearly and completely.

Completed

Answers the question asked

Responds to the literal request. Gets the job done technically but doesn't go beyond the instruction.

Excellent

Answers the question — and anticipates the next one

Thinks ahead. Notices what the person will need after they receive this. Includes it without being asked.

The Tool

The self-review checklist — use before you submit anything

Is the main point clear in the first sentence or the first glance? The person receiving this shouldn't have to search for what matters.

Have I checked for the one thing that could be wrong? Every piece of work has a weak point. Find it before someone else does.

Is this as concise as it can be? Cut anything that doesn't add meaning. Length is not thoroughness.

Does this do what it was supposed to do? Go back to the original goal — does this work actually achieve it?

Would I be comfortable if this had my name on it publicly? If the answer is "mostly" — it needs ten more minutes.

Tomorrow's Takeaway

Before you submit your next piece of work — run the checklist.

Five questions. Two minutes. Ask yourself: Is the main point clear? Have I checked the weak point? Is it as concise as it can be? Does it achieve the goal? Would I put my name on this? If the answer to any of them is "not quite" — take ten more minutes.
