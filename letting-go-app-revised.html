Gate 0 · First Things First
Is this a Process or a People problem?
The most common mistake is blaming the person when the system is broken — or staying because you think a process fix will solve a people problem.
Points to Process
  • Person performed well — then declined
  • Multiple people struggle with the same thing
  • No clear expectations were set
  • No training or tools were provided
  • Direction changes constantly
Points to People
  • Expectations were clear — still not met
  • Others in same environment succeed
  • Feedback given — no genuine change
  • Pattern across different roles or tasks
  • You've invested and tried — nothing moves
The Key Question

If I fixed the process tomorrow and gave this person full support — do I genuinely believe they could perform at the level this role requires?

Gate 0 · Result
Fix the system first.
This looks like a process problem. Letting someone go won't fix what's broken — it'll put a new person into the same broken environment.
Recommendation

Set clear expectations, provide the right tools and training, and give a fair opportunity to perform. Then reassess. If the problem persists after a fair system is in place — you'll have your answer and a clean conscience.

Remember

Process fixes output. It does not fix will, character, or fit. But make sure you've given the system a fair chance — otherwise you'll keep cycling through people without solving the real problem.

Gate 0 · Result
Two things are true at once.
The process needed work — and this person still isn't the right fit for the role going forward. That's a valid and honest position.
Your Path Forward

Fix the system for the next person. Make the transition now. The broken process doesn't change the outcome for this individual — it means the conversation is honest: the role is changing and the requirements have changed with it.

You are not punishing them for a broken system. You're being honest that even with improvements, this role needs a different skill level or capability.
In the conversation, acknowledge the environment had issues — and still be clear the role needs something different going forward.
Fix the process before or alongside hiring the next person. Don't repeat the cycle.
Gate 1 · Timing Check
Is the timing concern real — or avoidance?
Sometimes waiting is the right call. Most of the time it's the most expensive delay you'll make.
✓ Legitimate — Plan toward it
  • No one to cover the role yet
  • Critical project — gap causes real damage
  • Not enough documentation to protect yourself
  • Transition needs 2–4 more weeks to set up
✗ Avoidance — Move now
  • "It's the holidays"
  • "They just had a baby"
  • "Things have been better lately"
  • "Maybe it'll resolve itself"
  • "I just feel bad"
The Question That Cuts Through

What specifically needs to happen before you can move?

If you can name a concrete condition — plan toward it. If you can't — it's not timing. It's fear.

Gate 1 · Result
Plan now. Move then.
You have a legitimate reason to wait. Valid. But the plan starts today.
Action Required

Name the condition. Set the date. A decision without a deadline is not a decision — it's a feeling you carry that costs energy every single day.

Every month you delay: your best people watch you tolerate it. The problem gets worse. The conversation gets harder.
Use the waiting period to prepare: document everything, identify who covers the role, confirm logistics.
When the condition is met — move immediately. Don't find a new reason to wait.

Step 3 · The Guilt Check
Do you feel like you'd be hurting them?
The most common place owners get stuck. Let's work through it.
❌ Question Keeping You Stuck

"What will happen to them if I let them go?"

✓ Question That Moves You Forward

"What is happening to my team and business by keeping them?"

Keeping the wrong person is not kindness. It's avoidance dressed up as compassion.
Your team sees it. It silently lowers the standard for everyone watching.
The person often knows something is off. The uncertainty is its own kind of cruelty.
You are not responsible for their life. You're responsible for being honest, fair, and clear.
Letting someone go with dignity is an act of respect — not harm.
