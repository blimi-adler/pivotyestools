Module 03 Understanding What's Needed

Know the Goal

Busy isn't the same as useful. The most important thing you can do before you start working is understand what you're actually working toward.

~25 minTo complete
Read · Reflect · WriteFormat
1 TakeawayTo use tomorrow

The Core Idea

The difference between the task and the goal

Every task has two layers. The first layer is the instruction — what you were told to do. The second layer is the goal — why it matters, what it's supposed to produce, what problem it's solving. Most people operate entirely at the first layer. They complete the instruction and call it done.

The people who move fastest and make the biggest impact operate at the second layer. They understand the goal well enough that when the instruction turns out to be wrong — or incomplete, or outdated — they can adjust without needing to be retold everything. Goal-level thinking is what separates execution from contribution.

"Perfect execution of the wrong task is still failure. Know what you're solving for before you start solving."

The Two Levels

Task thinking vs. goal thinking

Here's what the difference looks like in practice — same situation, two different ways of showing up.

Task Level
Follows the instruction exactly as given
Waits to be told what to do next. Completes the task, reports back. If something is unclear, either guesses or gets stuck.
"You asked me to send the report — I sent it."
Goal Level
Understands what the task is in service of
Asks what's needed before starting. Flags when the task won't achieve the goal. Brings context and judgment, not just effort.
"The report is sent. I noticed one number looked off — I flagged it before you present."

The Tool

Four questions that reveal the real goal

The Goal Clarifier

Ask these before you execute anything significant.

1 What's this for? Not "what is it" — but why does it exist? Who will use it? What decision does it inform?
2 What does a good outcome look like — specifically? Not "done well" but what you'd see, hear, or measure if it worked.
3 What would make this not useful? What would cause this work to miss even if it's technically complete? Knowing this early prevents late surprises.
4 Who else is affected by this? Understanding downstream impact changes how you approach the work — and what you flag along the way.

Go Deeper

Reflection

Reflection 1

"Have you ever completed something perfectly and still felt like you missed the point? What was actually missing?"

Reflection 2

"In your current role, how often do you know the 'why' behind what you're asked to do? What would change if you always did?"

Reflection 3

"What's one area in your work where you could shift from task-level to goal-level thinking — starting this week?"

Tomorrow's Takeaway

Before your next task — write the goal in one sentence.

"The goal here is to ___." Say it to the person who gave you the work. If your sentence matches what they meant, you're aligned. If it doesn't — you just saved yourself a lot of wasted effort.
