Gate 0 · First Things First
Is this a Process problem or a People problem?
Before anything else — make sure you're solving the right thing. The most common mistake is blaming the person when the system is broken.
Process Problem
  • Person performed well — then declined
  • Multiple people struggle with the same thing
  • No clear expectations were ever set
  • No training or tools were provided
  • Direction changes constantly
People Problem
  • Expectations were clear — still not met
  • Others in same environment succeed
  • Feedback given — no genuine change
  • Pattern across different roles or tasks
  • You've tried everything — nothing moves
Ask Yourself

Have I given this person a fair environment to succeed in — and they still didn't?

Gate 0 · Result
Fix the system first.
Based on what you described, this looks like a process problem — not a people problem. Letting someone go won't fix what's broken.
Recommendation

Before making a people decision, address the environment. Set clear expectations, provide the right tools and training, and give a fair opportunity to perform. Then reassess. If the problem persists after that — you'll have your answer.

Remember

Process fixes output. It does not fix will, character, or fit. But make sure you've given the system a fair chance first — otherwise you'll keep cycling through people without solving the real problem.

Gate 1 · Timing Check
Is the timing concern real — or is it avoidance?
Sometimes waiting makes sense. Most of the time, it's the most expensive delay you'll make.
✓ Legitimate Timing
  • No one to cover the role yet
  • Critical project — gap causes real damage
  • Not enough documentation legally
  • Transition needs 2–4 more weeks
✗ This Is Avoidance
  • "It's the holidays"
  • "They just had a baby"
  • "Things have been better lately"
  • "Maybe it'll resolve itself"
  • "I just feel bad"
The Question That Cuts Through

When would be the right time? What specifically needs to happen first?

If you can't name a concrete condition — it's not timing. It's fear.

Gate 1 · Result
Plan now. Move then.
You have a legitimate reason to wait. That's valid. But the plan starts today — not when the condition is met.
Action Required

Name the condition. Set the date. A decision without a deadline is not a decision — it's a feeling you carry around. Decide right now: what needs to happen, and when will you move after it does?

Every month you delay: your best people watch you tolerate it. The problem gets worse. The conversation gets harder.
Use the waiting period to prepare: document the issues, identify who covers the role, confirm the logistics.
When the condition is met — move immediately. Don't find a new reason to wait.

Step 3 · The Guilt Check
Do you still feel like you'd be hurting them?
This is the most common place owners get stuck. Let's work through it.
❌ The Question Keeping You Stuck

"What will happen to them if I let them go?"

✓ The Question That Moves You Forward

"What is happening to my team and business by keeping them?"

Keeping the wrong person is not kindness. It's avoidance dressed up as compassion.
Your team sees it. It silently lowers the standard for everyone watching.
The person often knows something is off. The uncertainty is its own kind of cruelty.
You are not responsible for their life. You're responsible for being honest, fair, and clear.
Letting someone go with dignity is an act of respect — not harm.

Your Conversation Script
1

Open with the news — in the first 30 seconds

Don't lead with praise. Don't warm up. Say it immediately.

"I need to share some difficult news. We're letting you go, effective [date]."
2

Give one reason — tailored to your situation

Not a list. One clear reason. Never about their character.

"This is about the role and what the business needs."
3

Cover the logistics

Have all of this confirmed before you walk in. Last day, final pay, references, transition.

4

Let them respond

Don't fill the silence. Don't defend. They get to have a reaction. Give them the space.

5

End with respect

Acknowledge their contribution where it's real. Keep it short.

"I appreciate what you brought to this role. I wish you well in what comes next."
Before You Walk In
My decision is final — I am not here to negotiate
Final pay, last day, and severance (if any) are confirmed
I know who covers their responsibilities after they leave
Reference policy decided — I know what I'll say if asked
Private setting arranged — end of day or Friday preferred
I can say my one-sentence reason clearly and without stumbling
