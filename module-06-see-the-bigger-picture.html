The Core Idea

Mission and values aren't wall decorations

Most organizations have a mission statement. Most employees have read it once — maybe at orientation — and haven't thought about it since. That's a missed opportunity, because mission and values, when you actually internalize them, change how you make decisions every single day.

They answer the questions no job description can answer: How do I handle this situation no one prepared me for? What do I do when two things are competing for my attention? How do I treat this customer, this colleague, this problem? Values are the operating system underneath the rules. When you know them — really know them — you don't need someone to tell you what to do in every situation. You already know.

"Values aren't what you say when things are easy. They're what you do when things are hard and no one is watching."

And here's the communication piece: people who are anchored in the mission communicate differently. They explain their decisions in terms of what the organization is trying to build. They push back when something doesn't align. They bring ideas that move the mission forward — not just ideas that make their job easier. That's the kind of team member every organization needs more of.

Two Anchors

Mission vs. Values — what each one does

Mission

Why the organization exists

The mission is the direction. It answers: what are we building, who are we building it for, and why does it matter? It's what gets people out of bed in the morning beyond a paycheck. When you know the mission, you can point your work toward it — even when no one is directing you.

Values

How the organization operates

Values are the rules of the road. They answer: when two things compete, what wins? How do we treat people? What do we refuse to compromise on? Values make decisions predictable — and they make a team trustworthy, because everyone is working from the same code.

Mission in practice

"We help small businesses build structure so they can grow."

When you know this is the mission, you don't just complete tasks — you ask: does this actually help the client build something that lasts? That question changes your output.

Values in practice

"We're direct, honest, and we care about people."

When these are your values, you don't wait to be told to speak up. You don't sugarcoat hard feedback. You don't treat clients like transactions. The value drives the behavior.

Values in Action

What it looks like when values drive communication

Values only matter when they show up in behavior. Here's what value-driven communication actually looks like on the ground.

The Value What it looks like when it drives how you communicate
Honesty You say what's true even when it's uncomfortable. You don't soften feedback until it loses meaning. You flag problems early instead of hoping they go away.
Respect You give people your full attention. You don't dismiss ideas before hearing them out. You deliver hard feedback without contempt.
Excellence You say "I want to make sure this is right before I send it" and mean it. You don't submit work you know is below your standard just to hit a deadline.
Ownership When something goes wrong in your area, you say "that's mine — here's what happened and here's what I'm doing about it." No deflecting, no blame-shifting.
Care You notice when someone on the team is struggling and say something. You deliver information in a way that considers how the other person will receive it.
Growth You ask for feedback instead of waiting for a review. You say "I want to understand what I could have done better here" — and mean it.

The Tool

Three ways to communicate from the mission

Mission-Driven Communication

Show that you understand what this organization is building.

1 Name the mission when you explain your decisions. "I prioritized this because it directly serves what we're here to do." One sentence connects your work to the why. It signals that you think at that level.
2 Use values as your reasoning, not just your feelings. Instead of "I don't feel right about this," say: "This doesn't align with how we've committed to treating clients — I want to flag it before we move forward."
3 Bring ideas that serve the mission, not just your role. "I've been thinking about how we could do this in a way that better serves what we're building" — that's a different conversation than "here's how to make my job easier."
